# Heterogeneous Metadata Services team at an Ivy League institution

## Executive summary

A metadata-focused team at a U.S.-based Ivy League institution partnered with mathspp to deliver a 4-day, fully customised Python training for 10 participants with mixed levels of experience.
The primary goal was to _level the team's technical baseline_, introduce key software engineering concepts, and improve confidence in tackling real-world problems.

The results of the four days of training, comprised of interactive instruction through live-coding, hands-on exercises, and adaptive curriculum design, include:

 - Increased confidence across participants
 - Adoption of modern Python tooling in internal projects
 - Internal knowledge sharing beyond the training itself
 - A stronger shared foundation across previously heterogeneous skill levels

As the hiring manager summarised,

 > “From my perspective, it was definitely a good investment on multiple levels [...].” (T.T.)

## The client

The training was delivered to a metadata services-focused team within a large academic institution, alongside members of a second, collaborating team.
The participants were mainly characterised by:

 - **Highly diverse technical backgrounds**: some participants had less than one year of Python experience while others had 5+ years of experience
 - **Not a traditional software engineering background**: the participants were metadata professionals that did _not_ have a formal computer science or programming background

The lack of uniformity in Python knowledge and software engineering practices manifested itself in an uneven understanding of core concepts (like OOP) and gaps in software engineering best practices and tooling (collaboration on GitHub, testing, packaging).
But most importantly, this lack of uniformity meant it was difficult to set consistent expectations across the team and there was limited confidence in tackling more complex problems.

As such, the hiring manager identified a need for alignment, shared vocabulary, and foundational technical depth, and that's what we were hired to help with.

## Objectives

The training was aimed at **leveling the playing field**, ensuring all participants share exposure to key concepts and techniques, while also **increasing confidence and autonomy** by enabling participants to approach new problems more independently.
This was to be achieved by covering a number of **practical software engineering skills** during the training.

## Approach

We designed a **fully customised**, 4-day intensive training around three main topics:

 1. Object-oriented programming
 2. Testing fundamentals
 3. Modern Python tooling

These main topics were identified by working closely with the client and represented the topics that the participants struggled with the most or the skills that would bring more benefit to the teams in terms of productivity.

Having an **adaptive curriculum** rather than a rigid syllabus meant that the content was tailored to the team's needs beforehand and it allowed the content to be adjusted dynamically throughout the week based on participant feedback and observed difficulty.
This proved to be key, since the first day ended up being less successful due to a mismatch between the expected fluency of the participants and their actual fluency.
Due to the dynamic nature of the curriculum, difficulty was rebalanced immediately, the pacing was adjusted, and the practical exercises were aligned more closely with the real level of the participants.
When asked what aspects of the training they found most valuable, one participant indicated our “adaptability, flexibility, [...], and quick wits” (W.N.).

After this adjustment, we saw that participants engaged actively, the sessions became highly interactive, and the learning process was driven by questions and discussions.
At the end of the training, participants noted that “interactivity” (T.W.) or “the live questions and answers” (D.C.) were the parts of the training that were the most valuable.

The end of the training was designed to have a **high-impact final session**, where participants built and published a Python package and then installed and ran successfully the packages published by the others.
This provided a breakthrough moment where participants turned abstract concepts — like project management and packaging — into something tangible and usable:

 > “People had an **aha moment** when they were able to publish their project and run it... That worked for everyone.” (T.T.)

## Results

### Quantitative feedback

 - Overall rating: **4.75 / 5**
 - Session engagement: **4.5 / 5**
 - Clarity of explanations: **4.63 / 5**

### Increased confidence

Participants self-reported an **increase in their confidence**, allowing them to tackle unfamiliar problems, engage with new tools and techniques, and improving their ability to reason about code and design decisions.
**Every participant** was able to describe how they'll apply what they learned during the training to their daily work.
When interviewed 4 months after the training, the hiring manager reported

 > “I see greater confidence; the ability to take on new challenges.” (T.T.)

### Internal knowledge sharing

After the training, a group of participants organised an internal session where they presented the concepts and tooling around packaging, from the high-impact final session.
This indicates **retention of knowledge**, **confidence** in teaching others, and the **extended impact** of the training beyond the live sessions and the participants themselves.

### Alignment across the team

From a management perspective, the training created a shared baseline of concepts and vocabulary, making it easier to set expectations and to discuss technical work, as well as reducing the uncertainty that stems from the varied backgrounds of the team members.
Quoting the hiring manager,

 > “Just being in the same room walking incrementally through this set of lessons was very valuable. As a manager, it puts me at ease, in the sense that I can set my expectations more accurately because I know what everyone has been exposed to.” (T.T.)

### Long-term value

Based on the follow-up interview, our training delivered lasting benefits beyond the immediate outcomes, such as a foundation for **continued learning** and improvement, a model for future internal upskilling efforts, **improved collaboration through shared understanding**, and exposure to **practices that can be adopted progressively** (such as testing and packaging).

## Client and participant feedback

Based on the feedback provided by the participants after the training, the key themes included:

 - The training style was **engaging, flexible, and responsive**
 - The instructor's enthusiasm and curiosity made **complex topics accessbible**
 - The adaptive approach ensured that the content stayed relevant

**All** but one participant immediately showed interest in further advanced sessions and during the follow-up interview the hiring manager said

 > “I would definitely be interested in pursuing something like that again.” (T.T.)

## Conclusion

This case study demonstrates the impact of **adaptive, customised training**, strong emphasis on **hands-on learning**, and teaching that prioritises **understanding and engagement**.
Even in a fully online setting, the training delivered measurable satisfaction, observable behaviour changes, and lasting team-level improvements.
